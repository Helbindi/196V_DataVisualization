Andy Zhu

Dataset

MTGJSON is an open-source project that catalogs all Magic: The Gathering cards into a portable format. It is produced by a group of dedicated fans maintaining and supplying data from multiple sources and approved partners. Since quarantine started, I have been trying to get back into MTG since it has a free online platform (MTG Arena). MTG was first introduced in 1993, since then it has implemented and updated new rules and cards every year. There is a lot to process when first starting the game. I was curious whether visualizations created from this dataset could be useful to explaining the game.
There are over 20,000 unique cards in MTG, thus I am limited the dataset to the latest three sets in Standard because I am unfamiliar with the other formats and these three sets were released after I stopped playing, so I am interested in learning about them.

The data is available online at: MTGJSON

Initial Analysis Questions

  1. Outside of the rules of the game, what factors about the game may confuse or overwhelm the players?
  2. When new cards are released, are they evenly distributed? Are the developers trying to promote diversity?
  3. I heard getting into MTG can be expensive. Is it possible to play without spending large amounts of money?

Discoveries & Insights

Card Types in MTG

Permanents

Artifacts

Artifacts are permanents that represent magical items, animated constructs, pieces of equipment, or other inanimate objects and devices.

Creatures (Tribal)

Creatures may represent warriors, minions, beats and monsters that have an attack and toughness value. Thus they can attack the opponent and block the attacks of the opponent's creatures.

Enchantment

Enchantments represent persistent magical effects, usually remaining in play indefinitely. Most have continuous or triggered abilities, but some have abilities that can be activated by their controllers.

Land

Lands represent locations that the player controls which generate mana for the player to play other cards. You can only play one land on the player's turn unless stated otherwise.

Planeswalker

Planeswalkers represent powerful beings within the game world and thematically also represent identities of the player. As cards, Planeswalkers are played with an initial amount of loyalty counters which act as their in-game resource to survive or use their abilities. Playing a duplicate copy of a Planeswalker that is already on the board will destroy the initial one. Similar to the Legendary rule.

Non-permanents

Instant

Instants are one-shot or short-term magical spells that the player can use at any time including on the opponent's turn.

Sorcery

Like Instants, Sorcery are one-shot or short-term magical spells but they can only be played on the player's main phase and only when nothing else is on the stack.

Dual Colors in MTG

White-Blue

White-Blue is slow and steady. Typical white-blue decks stall the game and let the users cast their major spells in the late game.

Common mechanics: Flying, Flash, tapping, effects that prevent creatures from attacking or blocking, returning permanents to hand, counterspells, temporarily exiling permanents

Common creature types: Birds, Kithkin, Wizards

Blue-Black

Blue-black is related to secrets and forbidden knowledge. Blue's emphasis on information and black's solitary nature combine to create a very secretive color pair. The opponents of Blue-Black decks often realize that they are going to lose when it is too late.

Common mechanics: Milling, card draw, discard, library manipulation, casting from graveyard, large creatures with big drawbacks, unblockability

Common creature types: Merfolk, Faeries, Rogues, Assassins, Wizards

Ravnica guild: House Dimir

Black-Red

Black's anti-sociality and Red's hedonism combine to create a very sadistic and sociopathic color pair. Black-red decks typically prefer overwhelming opponents at all cost, often at the expense of their own creatures and even their users' life total.

Common mechanics: Haste, Wither, spells and creature that are undercosted but have drawbacks hurt the casters, sacrifice, direct damage/life loss, disallowing life gain, power boosting, +X/-X effects

Common creature types: Demons, Elementals, Goblins, Devils

Deck

A regular deck needs a minimum of 60 cards but there is no maximum restriction to deck size. A deck can consists of any combination of permanents or non-permanents but you can only have a maximum of 4 copies of a card except for basic lands and a few exceptions.


Turn 1 represents the probability of your starting hand (7 cards). While sequential turns represent your hand after a single draw afterwards (7 + n draws).

  • Four copies is generally best for cards you want in almost every situation. The bread and butter of the deck.
  • Three copies is generally best for cards you want to play but comes with some restrictions(Legendary rule, Planeswalker, High cost).
  • Two copies is for cards that are nice to have in some situations but never every or has restrictions(High cost, etc).

Looking at all three sets, The majority of cards are between CMC 2 and 4 regardless of color.

Boxplot: Prices among rarity types of a specific Standard set.
From the boxplot, it is clear that rarity plays a role a cards price. Amongst these different rarity, their prices are not the same for their rarity counterparts. Disregarding outliers, the prices of cards typically cluster into three groups: low, medium, high.

It is also shown that the majority of cards are actually in the low cost category. While they may not be as good as the other higher cost cards, they are playable on a leveled field. One such way is a special format called Pauper. It has the same rules such as deck size and copy limit but the cards in the deck must all be Common rarity. Also if the player is not playing in a competitive environment but with a small group, rules can be applied to deck building such as limiting the price of a total deck, no Mythic or Rare cards, or no cards over a specific price. This is a way to play MTG without worrying about the cost.


This is the price history of Lurrus of the Dream Den from the Theros Beyond Death set. This card cost twice as much as other rare cards in the same set. On April 18th, The price was around the same as the other rare cards but by the 20th, the card now cost twice as much. The reason is due to demand - after the 18th, people started to realized the potential and strengths of this card as shown in the increased appearances of a deck featuring this card called Rakdos Lurrus in a MTG Tournaments.

Summary

For new players, they can be overwhelmed by the amount of card types and color combinations and how they work. Not only just learning about the cards but there is also the challenge (and fun) in making their own deck. Players need to consider what card and how many of them to add into their decks. Players are given a large amount of freedom in how their decks turn out. Each set introduces around 300 new cards where all types of cards and colors are represented. This also applies to how they play their decks in-game. A large majority of the cards are below 4 CMC. Thus, players are allowed to have multiple options in what types of cards they and their opponent can play early on in a game. There are cards that are very expensive but there are also cards that are extremely cheap in comparison. Although the cheaper cards may not be as good as the expensive ones, there are a lot of ways to incorporate rules or formats where using cheaper cards can be just as fun and engaging as expensive ones. As a result, people can enjoy playing MTG without having to worry about the cost.
